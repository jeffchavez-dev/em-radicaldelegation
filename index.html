Leverage and Engagement Managers

Chapter 2 — Understanding the EM role you're growing into

Summary

Most assistants are treated as task-runners. The book argues that a great assistant should become an Engagement Manager (EM) — a hybrid of skilled EA and executive chief of staff. As an EM, you don't just handle tasks your VC Partner hands you. You proactively own their inbox, manage their relationships, prepare them for every meeting, track every commitment, and free them to focus only on high-leverage work. The goal: make your VC Partner the most trusted, prepared, and effective leader in their network — because of how you operate behind the scenes.

The Problem — What your VC Partner (CEO/Founder) is struggling with

Most executives are drowning in low-leverage work that any skilled person could handle. They spend their days context-switching between scheduling emails, writing follow-ups, chasing logistics, and putting out fires — leaving almost no time for the strategic, high-value work only they can do. The result:

  • They feel busy but not productive — always reactive, rarely proactive
  • They lack the cognitive bandwidth to think clearly about their most important decisions
  • They're too in the weeds to build the leadership presence and reputation they want
  • They hired an assistant — but are still doing most things themselves because they haven't learned how to truly delegate

eDrafting

Chapter 3 — You turn their 30-second voice note into a sent email

Summary

After every meeting, your VC Partner pulls out their phone and dictates a rough 30-second voice note — who they met, what stood out, any next steps. That's all they do. You receive the dictation, run it through the 7-part follow-up email template, use ChatGPT to polish it, and send a personalized, professional email in their voice — often within hours. Your VC Partner never writes a follow-up email again. You own the entire pipeline from dictation to sent email.

The Problem — What your VC Partner (CEO/Founder) is struggling with

After every meeting, most executives mean to send a follow-up email — but rarely do. The moment passes, the details blur, and the connection fades. When they do write one, it's generic and takes 20 minutes they don't have. The result:

  • Commitments made in meetings silently drop — no article sent, no intro made, no follow-through
  • Relationships that could compound into deals, partnerships, or referrals simply don't — because the follow-up never came
  • They come across as forgettable, even when the meeting itself was great
  • Writing emails feels like a tax on their time — so they avoid it, and connections go cold

Action plan — how you run the eDrafting system

1
Technical setup — install the app, build the landing pad, set up draft labels You (EA/EM)

Three things to set up before the first dictation ever arrives:

Full technical setup — app · landing pad · draft labels

1A — App install (your VC Partner's side)

Recommended: Otter.ai (iOS / Android) — Transcribes voice in real time, shareable via link or copy-paste — Alternatively: iPhone native "Dictation" button (mic icon on keyboard) or Android voice recorder — Goal: one tap, start talking, done. No friction. Send your VC Partner this one-line instruction: "Install Otter.ai, tap record right after any meeting, say what you remember, send it to [the landing pad]. That's all you need to do."

1B — Landing pad (your setup)

The landing pad is one dedicated inbox where ALL dictations arrive. Your VC Partner should only ever send to this one place — never hunt for where to send it. Options: • Dedicated Slack DM between you and your VC Partner (easiest) • A shared WhatsApp or iMessage thread labeled "Dictations" • A dedicated email alias (e.g., dictations@[yourdomain].com → forwards to your inbox) Set it up, share the details with your VC Partner, and confirm: "From now on, send all dictations here. I'll take it from there." Label it clearly in your own inbox so you process landing pad messages first every morning.

1C — Draft labels: "Ready to Send" and "Need Your Input"

After you draft the follow-up email, you place it in one of two states before sending to your VC Partner for review: 📗 READY TO SEND Use when: you're confident the email is complete and accurate. What to tell your VC Partner: "This is ready — just reply 'send' or hit send in Drafts." Subject line prefix you add: [READY TO SEND] Follow-up: [Name] | [Company] 📙 NEED YOUR INPUT Use when: you need a decision, a clarification, or a detail only they know. What to tell your VC Partner: "I need one thing from you before this goes out — see below." Subject line prefix you add: [NEED YOUR INPUT] Follow-up: [Name] | [Company] Rule: never send a "Need Your Input" email to the final recipient without getting an answer first. Rule: once your VC Partner consistently approves "Ready to Send" drafts unchanged, escalate to sending autonomously. Create these as Gmail labels / Outlook categories now, before the first dictation arrives.
2
Build and maintain the 7-part email template You (EA/EM)

Create a shared Google Doc with the anatomy below. You use this as the base for every follow-up email. Your job is to fill in the gaps from the rough dictation — your VC Partner doesn't need to cover every part perfectly.

The 7-part email anatomy with examples

Part I — Header (this is for you only — not in the email)

Who: [Full name — phonetic spelling if unusual, so you get it right] Priority: High / Normal High = send before end of day Normal = within 24 hours is fine Send by: [Day + time] Future pace: Y / N — should you offer to schedule a next meeting in the email? Sign-off word: e.g., "Cheers" — when you hear this, the dictation is done

Part II — Greeting

"Hey Sachin, it was great to connect. I really enjoyed getting to know you and learning a little more about what you're doing at Catalyst."

Part III — Positive Impact (be specific — your job is to make this concrete from what the VC Partner mentioned)

"I don't think I've ever met someone with such a grounded view of the future of AI. Your vision for automated vehicles is truly fascinating." Note: No matter how routine the meeting, there's always something positive. If the dictation is vague ("it was a good meeting"), follow up with your VC Partner to get one specific detail before drafting.

Part IV — Common Ground

"Beyond that, I'm thrilled to meet another craft beer enthusiast. It's not often I meet someone who has explored more of the Bay's microbreweries than I have. I'm really looking forward to checking out Drake's Brewery in San Leandro."

Part V — Key Takeaways (you extract commitments from the dictation and format as bullets)

"Here's the key next steps as I remember them: - I promised to send you the link to Paul Graham's maker's vs manager's time article. [You: find and hyperlink this before sending] - You offered to introduce me to your co-founder Eric if he's open to it. - If I missed anything, please let me know."

Part VI — Future-Pace (if VC Partner said Y on future pacing)

"Otherwise, I've CC'd [your name] to help us find a time to connect sometime next month."

Part VII — Sign-Off (always the same — this is your VC Partner's "I'm done dictating" signal to you)

"Cheers, [VC Partner name]"

Meeting Debriefs

Chapter 4 — You turn their debrief dictation into a relationship superpower

Summary

After every meaningful meeting, your VC Partner dictates a quick debrief to you using the APPA format — Action Items, Professional Challenges, Personal Interests & Passions, About/Facts. You convert this into a rich contact profile in a CRM, execute or track every action item, and — before every future meeting with that person — send your VC Partner a Meeting Prebrief: a one-page brief so they walk in fully prepared without doing any prep work themselves. The result: your VC Partner becomes the most trustworthy, prepared, and memorable person in any room — because of the system you built and run.

Core concepts

APPA FormatAction Items · Professional Challenges · Personal Interests & Passions · About/Facts
Meeting DebriefYour VC Partner's quick post-meeting dictation — you extract and store everything
Meeting PrebriefYou send this to your VC Partner before every future meeting with that person
Loop LeverageYou track every commitment until confirmed closed — by either party
Slim CommitmentsLow-effort actions you execute independently (send article, draft intro email)
True CommitmentsHigher-stakes promises only your VC Partner can fulfill — you remind and prep them
The Problem — What your VC Partner (CEO/Founder) is struggling with

Executives meet dozens of people every month — but retain almost none of it. Names blur, context evaporates, and commitments made in meetings fall through the cracks. The result is a network that doesn't compound:

  • They walk into follow-up meetings with almost no memory of what was discussed before — it shows
  • They promised to make an intro, send a resource, or follow up — and never did. Trust erodes.
  • Personal details (a sick child, a marathon, a career pivot) are forgotten — so every conversation resets to zero instead of deepening
  • Their CRM (if they even have one) is outdated, incomplete, and never used before meetings

Action plan — how you run the Meeting Debrief system

1
Set up the CRM You (EA/EM)

Pick a CRM your VC Partner already trusts or one you can manage independently (Notion, Salesforce, HubSpot, or Clay). Create an APPA contact template. Import your VC Partner's existing contacts from their email and calendar as the starting database.

2

Make a simple card or phone note your VC Partner can glance at after meetings. The simpler you make it for them, the more consistently they'll use it. Their job is to talk — your job is to structure everything.

Coach your VC Partner: the "About/Facts" section is the most powerful long-term. When you include "Ask about her daughter's marathon training" in next month's Prebrief, and they ask — nobody expects that level of care. That's what makes them unforgettable.

Inbox Shadowing

Chapter 5 — You own their inbox so they never have to

Summary

Inbox Shadowing means you proactively manage your VC Partner's entire inbox on their behalf. You build this in four phases: first you Triage (label every email), then you draft responses for their review, then you reach full Shadowing where you handle most emails autonomously. Your VC Partner checks their inbox only 3 times a day. The deeper value: you use their inbox + CRM to proactively send relationship emails they'd never find time to write — turning their network into a compounding asset.

Core concepts

Inbox TriagingYou label every incoming email before your VC Partner sees it
Please HandleYou respond fully — your VC Partner never sees it
FYIYour VC Partner should know this exists, but doesn't need to respond
UrgentRequires their direct attention — you escalate via Slack/text
Everything Else FolderAll triaged email lives here; their inbox stays near-zero
Proactive OutreachYou initiate relationship touch-points using inbox + CRM — without being asked
The Problem — What your VC Partner (CEO/Founder) is struggling with

The average CEO spends 13+ hours a week on email — a quarter of their working life. Their inbox is a never-ending stream of requests, updates, and noise they can't escape. The result:

  • They're constantly pulled into reactive mode — responding to whoever emailed last instead of focusing on what matters most
  • Important relationship emails (check-ins, thank-yous, personal follow-ups) never get written because there's "no time"
  • They feel guilty about slow replies but can't keep up — so they batch-ignore, then batch-respond poorly
  • Even with an EA, they're still manually triaging everything themselves — because no one set up a real system

Action plan — how you build Inbox Shadowing for your VC Partner

Phase 1 — Setup (Week 1)

1
Get full inbox access You (EA/EM) + VC Partner

Ask your VC Partner to grant you Google Workspace delegate access or Outlook shared mailbox access. Set up your escalation channel (Slack DM or text) for anything that truly can't wait — this is your Urgent line.

2
Create the label structure You (EA/EM)

You set up 3 labels in Gmail (or folders in Outlook): "Please Handle," "FYI," "Urgent" + an "Everything Else" archive folder. Optionally create Gmail filters to auto-sort newsletters and receipts straight to Everything Else.

What each label means — with real examples

Please Handle — you respond, your VC Partner never sees it

Use when: you can draft and send a complete, appropriate response without their input. Examples of emails you can handle: — A scheduling request from a former colleague reaching out to catch up — An investor contact asking if your VC Partner would be open to a connection — A project update where the response is obvious from context — Introduction requests you can facilitate on their behalf — Simple yes/no questions where their position is already clear to you

FYI — your VC Partner reads it, no response needed from them

Use when: they should know about this, but no action is required from them. Examples: — A portfolio company founder sharing a product update — A newsletter or article relevant to their investment thesis — A team member introducing themselves to the firm — A service provider announcing a new feature

Urgent — requires their direct input (should be rare)

Use when: only your VC Partner can respond. Escalate via your agreed channel immediately. Examples: — A portfolio CEO asking for their direct take on a board decision — A legal or compliance matter requiring their acknowledgment — A sensitive LP or co-investor situation only they can handle If you find yourself labeling too many things Urgent, revisit the definition together — it likely means you're not yet confident enough in the "Please Handle" scope.
3
Define "Please Handle" scope together You (EA/EM) + VC Partner

Spend one session walking through 10–15 real emails from their inbox and classifying each one together. This calibration sets your confidence level. The more context you have about their relationships and preferences, the wider your "Please Handle" scope can grow.
