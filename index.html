Leverage and Engagement Managers

Chapter 2 — What an EA becomes, and why it matters

Summary

Most people treat an EA as a time-saver — offloading small stuff. That's thinking too small. The book's argument is that your EA should become an Engagement Manager (EM): a hybrid between a highly skilled executive assistant and an executive chief of staff. An EM doesn't just save you time — they transform how you lead. They own your inbox, manage your relationships, prepare you for every meeting, and free you to spend time only on what moves the company. The goal: make you the legendary leader your company deserves — someone others trust, believe in, follow, and support.

Action plan — turning your EA into an EM

1
Audit what you're doing that your EA should own You

Review your calendar for the past 2 weeks. Every task where your value-add is minimal (scheduling, logistics, follow-ups, travel) moves to your EA immediately. This is the first step toward elevating them to EM.

Low-leverage tasks to hand off immediately

• Scheduling and rescheduling meetings • Travel booking and logistics • Responding to meeting invites and confirmations • Sending follow-up emails after meetings (→ eDrafting, Ch 3) • Researching contacts before meetings (→ Prebriefs, Ch 4) • Tracking action items and commitments • Managing your inbox (→ Inbox Shadowing, Ch 5) • Sending thank-you and acknowledgment emails • Recurring admin (expense reports, calendar hygiene)

An Engagement Manager (EM) is a unique role created to help executives unleash massive leverage. Think of them as a hybrid between a highly skilled executive assistant and an executive chief of staff. An EM's responsibilities include: 1. Owning any item, anywhere, that you can't execute as effectively as possible — from the obvious (scheduling, travel) to the nuanced (preparing you for difficult meetings, coaching follow-up after commitments, managing your inbox). 2. Building meaningful relationships with the most important people in your network — helping you build a trust base of people who will follow you through each step of your career.

eDrafting

Chapter 3 — Never write another follow-up email again

Summary

After every meeting you leave intending to follow up. You almost never do. eDrafting fixes this permanently. You dictate a 40-second voice note immediately after the meeting. Your EM receives it, runs it through a 7-part template (with ChatGPT if needed), and sends a polished, personalized follow-up email — often within hours, in your voice. Your only job is to talk for 40 seconds. Your EM handles everything else.

Core concepts

eDraftingYou dictate, your EM drafts and sends. You never write a follow-up email again.
40-Second Follow-UpA rough voice note right after the meeting — imperfect is fine
7-Part Email AnatomyHeader · Greeting · Positive Impact · Common Ground · Key Takeaways · Future-Pace · Sign-Off
Priority Dictation LabelTells your EM: Priority Level, Send Timestamp, Future Pacing Y/N, Sign-Off word
Specificity is KingThe more specific your Positive Impact and Common Ground, the better the email
AI DraftingYour EM uses ChatGPT to turn rough dictations into polished emails

Action plan — implementing eDrafting with your EM

1
Your EM sets up the dictation pipeline EM

Your EM creates a dedicated inbox (email or Slack channel) for all dictations, gets ChatGPT access, and sets you up with a voice-to-text app (phone native or Otter.ai).

2
Your EM builds the 7-part email template EM

Your EM maintains a shared Google Doc with the anatomy below. Every follow-up email must hit all 7 parts — your EM fills in the gaps from your rough dictation.

The 7-part email anatomy with examples

Part I — Header (for your EM only, not in the email)

Who: [Full name — spell phonetically if unusual] Priority: High / Normal Send by: [Day + time, e.g., "today by 5pm"] Future pace: Y / N — does your EM need to offer to schedule the next meeting? Sign-off word: e.g., "Cheers" — signals to your EM that the dictation is done

Part II — Greeting

"Hey Sachin, it was great to connect. I really enjoyed getting to know you and learning a little more about what you're doing at Catalyst."

Part III — Positive Impact (be specific — not just "great meeting")

"I don't think I've ever met someone with such a grounded view of the future of AI. Your vision for automated vehicles is truly fascinating." Tip: No matter how routine the meeting, there's always something positive. Even for a contentious meeting: "I really appreciate the way you and I can have an open discussion about these things, even when we disagree."

Part IV — Common Ground

"Beyond that, I'm thrilled to meet another craft beer enthusiast. It's not often I meet someone who has explored more of the Bay's microbreweries than I have. I'm really looking forward to checking out Drake's Brewery in San Leandro."

Part V — Key Takeaways

"Here's the key next steps as I remember them: - I offered to send you the link to Paul Graham's maker's vs manager's time article. [EM: please find and include this] - You offered to introduce me to your co-founder Eric if he's open to it. - If you think I left out anything important, please let me know."

Part VI — Future-Pace

"Otherwise, I've CC'd [EM name] to help us find a time to connect sometime next month."

Part VII — Sign-Off (always the same — it's your EM's "stop recording" signal)

"Cheers, [Your name]"

Priority label format

Meeting Debriefs

Chapter 4 — How to be the most trustworthy person in anyone's network

Summary

After every meaningful meeting, you dictate a structured debrief to your EM using the APPA format — Action Items, Professional Challenges, Personal Interests & Passions, About/Facts. Your EM converts this into a rich contact profile in a CRM, executes or tracks every action item, and — before every future meeting with that person — generates a Meeting Prebrief so you walk in fully prepared. The result: you become the most trustworthy, prepared, and memorable person in any room.

Core concepts

APPA FormatAction Items · Professional Challenges · Personal Interests & Passions · About/Facts
Meeting DebriefStructured dictation you give your EM right after a meeting
Meeting PrebriefBrief your EM prepares for you before every future meeting with that person
Loop LeverageYour EM tracks every commitment until confirmed closed — by either party
Slim CommitmentsLow-effort actions your EM executes independently (send article, make intro)
True CommitmentsHigher-stakes promises only you can fulfill — your EM reminds and preps you

Action plan — implementing Meeting Debriefs with your EM

1
Your EM sets up the CRM EM

Your EM picks and configures a CRM (Notion, Salesforce, HubSpot, or Clay) with an APPA contact template and imports existing contacts from your email and calendar.

2
Your EM creates your debrief dictation cheat sheet EM

A simple card or phone note you always have with you — the exact script to follow so you never forget a category.

The APPA debrief dictation script

What you say into your phone right after the meeting

"Hey [EM name], this is a meeting debrief for [Full Name — spell it out]. This is a [Priority Level] debrief. Action Items: — [Every commitment either of you made — be specific] Professional Challenges: — [Career goals, frustrations, things they're working through professionally] Personal Interests and Passions: — [Hobbies, family, personal projects, things they're excited about outside work] About/Facts: — [Anything miscellaneous — health updates, family news, upcoming life events, fun facts] If I missed or misunderstood anything, please let me know. Otherwise, please add these to their contact record. Cheers, [Your name]"
The "About/Facts" section is the most powerful long-term. When you ask "How's your daughter's marathon training going?" at the next meeting — nobody expects that. It's what makes people feel truly seen, and what separates a good networker from a legendary one.

Dictate within 10 minutes of the meeting ending. Your EM updates the CRM and sends back an acknowledgment confirming the record was captured.

4

From every debrief, your EM categorizes action items, executes what they can, and tracks everything else until closed. You receive a weekly open commitments digest.

How your EM classifies every action item
Handled by You: — Tasks only you can do (dictate a blog post, make a strategic call) — Your EM creates the task and follows up if not done within 48 hours Handled by Your EM — Slim Commitments: — Tasks your EM can execute independently (find + send an article, draft an intro email) — Your EM acts immediately after receiving the debrief — no check-in needed Loop Leverage: — Commitments either party made to the other (e.g., "I'll introduce you to Daniel") — Your EM creates a recurring reminder and follows up on your behalf until confirmed closed — If the other party hasn't followed through, your EM proactively nudges them for you

Inbox Shadowing

Chapter 5 — How to free yourself from your inbox once and for all

Summary

Inbox Shadowing means your EM proactively manages your entire inbox on your behalf. It builds in four phases: your EM first Triages (labels every email), then drafts responses for review, then reaches full Shadowing where they handle most emails autonomously. You check your inbox only 3 times a day. The deeper value: your EM uses your inbox + CRM to proactively send relationship emails you'd never find time to write — turning your network into a living, breathing asset.

Core concepts

Inbox TriagingYour EM labels every incoming email before you see it
Please HandleYour EM responds fully — you never see it
FYIYou should know this exists, but no response needed from you
UrgentRequires your direct attention — should be rare
Everything Else FolderAll triaged email lives here; your inbox stays near-zero
Proactive OutreachYour EM initiates relationship touch-points using inbox + CRM — without you asking

Action plan — building Inbox Shadowing with your EM

Phase 1 — Setup (Week 1)

1
Grant your EM full inbox access You

Google Workspace delegate access or Outlook shared mailbox. Agree on an escalation channel (Slack DM or text) for anything that truly can't wait.

2
Your EM creates the label structure EM

3 labels in Gmail (or folders in Outlook): "Please Handle," "FYI," "Urgent" + an "Everything Else" archive folder. Optional: Gmail filters to auto-sort newsletters and receipts straight to Everything Else.

What each label means — with real examples

Please Handle — your EM responds, you never see it

Use when: your EM can draft and send a complete response without your input. Examples: — A scheduling request from a former colleague reaching out to catch up — An investor asking if you'd be open to connecting with a portfolio CEO — A project status update where the answer is obvious from context — Introduction requests your EM can facilitate on your behalf — Simple yes/no questions where your position is already known

FYI — you read it, no response needed

Use when: you should know about this, but no action is required. Examples: — A team member introducing themselves — A service provider announcing a new feature — A newsletter or article genuinely relevant to your work — A project status your team is already handling

Urgent — requires your direct input (should be rare)

Use when: only you can respond. If your EM labels too many Urgent, revisit the definition together in your weekly feedback session. Examples: — Your CEO asking for your take on a specific decision — A legal or compliance matter requiring your acknowledgment — A sensitive personnel issue only you can address
3
Define "Please Handle" scope together You + EM

One focused session: walk through 10–15 real emails from your inbox and classify each one together. This calibration session sets the foundation for everything that follows.

Phase 2 — Inbox Triaging (Weeks 1–2)

4
Your EM triages only — no drafting yet EM

Your EM labels every incoming email. You check inbox 3x/day and correct any misclassifications. No drafting yet — this phase is purely about building labeling accuracy.

15 minutes every week: review 5–10 recent labeling decisions together. You explain reasoning when your EM got one wrong. Goal: 90%+ labeling accuracy within 2 weeks.

Phase 3 — Proactive Drafting (Weeks 3–4)

6
Your EM starts drafting responses EM

Your EM drafts replies for "Please Handle" emails and places them in Drafts with prefix "[DRAFT - Please review]." You review, edit minimally, and send — or approve for your EM to send directly.

Voice note you send when you want a specific email handled a specific way

The three-stage drafting progression

Stage 1 — Supervised drafts (Weeks 3–4): Your EM drafts → places in Drafts → you review → you or EM sends Stage 2 — Autonomous drafts (Week 5+): Your EM drafts and sends "Please Handle" independently Flags anything unusual or sensitive for your awareness Stage 3 — Full Inbox Shadowing: Your EM proactively manages the inbox and initiates outreach You check 3x/day max and only handle Urgent items yourself
